Personal Statement
MPhil in Economics University of Oxford
Crucially, I believe Oxford's MPhil in Economics is the ideal next step for enhancing my rigour in public sector microeconometric research. At a time when public spending is increasingly constrained, I aspire to use data to guide policy decisions efficiently. As a candidate, I believe my past and ongoing research initiatives best demonstrate my suitability, complemented by a track record of consistent First-Class honours as a College Scholar. I will first expand on my personal research before concluding with how my ambitions align particularly well with Oxford's programme.
The challenges and assumptions underlying cost-benefit analyses have always intrigued me, prompting me to investigate often-neglected second-order effects that could lead to substantial policy misallocations. For my undergraduate thesis, under the supervision of Professor Bhat- tacharya, I focused on the controversial case of HS2. Using STATA, I treated the November 2021 extension cancellation as a quasi-experiment to study its impact on the property market in Leeds. Using a Difference-in-Differences (DiD) approach, I compared property values near and far from the proposed HS2 construction site and identified significant losses of positive spillover effects. Specifically, properties in the cancellation area experienced an 8% greater decline in value compared to controls, although public discourse and compensation were primarily fo- cused on negative spillovers. For me, this underlines the need for governments to continually backtest estimated effects to ensure policies are based on the most accurate cost-benefit ratios. Although the DiD approach has its strengths, further reading on its validity left me increasingly concerned. Inspired by Helen Miller's compelling lecture series on price controls in develop- ing nations at the Marshall Society, I sought to extend the DiD framework during my gap-year investigation of petrol price caps in Jakarta. I integrated a regression discontinuity design with dynamic DiD to address the parallel trends assumption more plausibly and to derive a treatment effect profile rather than a single-point estimate. Specifically, I exploited geographical discon- tinuities in petrol price caps in the 'Badan Pusat' dataset to examine the impact on firms in an oligopolistic market. Preliminary findings suggest that while price controls reduce short-term price volatility, they increase market concentration and discourage long-term investment.

Although external validity across borders is challenging to establish, these findings prompted me to consider the secondary effects of a related intervention closer to home - the UK Energy Price Guarantee. I authored an article for CAMSIF, where I had previously served as chief editor, critically examining whether the guarantee introduced unnecessary market distortions or represented an optimal response to structural inequities. Drawing on Ramsey's elegant optimal taxation theory, I argued that such a policy diverged starkly from optimality, since taxation in an inelastic market such as energy should be high, not negative, to maximise revenue per unit of deadweight loss. However, in the second-best reality of political and distributional constraints, I explored how the assumptions behind this framework falter. I concluded that these frictions likely outweigh Jakarta-like underinvestment effects, even if accounted for. Writing this article allowed me to combine qualitative and quantitative approaches to deconstruct a policy premise, an exercise that I hope to continue at the postgraduate level.
Looking ahead, I think the Oxford programme aligns perfectly with my ambitions. With a strong desire to use empirical research to critique the theoretical models underpinning public policy, I find it essential to gain a rigorous grounding in advanced mathematical methods, mak- ing the two-year structure particularly appealing. I am especially drawn to the Industrial Organ- isation module, which combines my interests in game theory and econometrics. For example, I was fascinated by Hausman's (1996) application of the "Almost Ideal Demand System" to es- timate demand equations and determine optimal entry prices for new products. His innovative use of prices in other markets as Hausman-Nevo instruments to address endogeneity exempli- fies the kind of rigorous empirical work I aspire to undertake. I look forward to participating in such intellectually stimulating discussions with the Empirical Microeconomics research group and to exploring potential research assistantships.
Furthermore, I am particularly interested in Professor Bond's research on dynamic panel data models and would welcome the opportunity to explore their application to analyse firm be- haviour under regulatory constraints. Building on my current research, my aim is to incorporate such dynamic methods into my thesis to study long-term investment decisions in regulated markets, with the goal of informing more robust government policies that anticipate private investment reactions.
Beyond my research aspirations, I am also motivated by the prospect of teaching responsibili- ties. As a current supervisor in econometrics for second-year Cambridge students, I have found that discussing even foundational topics deepens my understanding of not only the material but also the motivations behind its development. Communicating complex economic concepts through teaching is something I hope to continue as a postgraduate.

Personal Statement
Economics and Finance Programme
University of Hong Kong
My interest in Economics, Finance and Mathematics was sparked through involvement in my family's retail business. Discussions with my dad about government policies and analysing monthly sales reports using mathematical models have fuelled my desire to study this interdisciplinary degree.
During my internship in a local company, I gained a basic understanding of the importance of the Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC) value in assessing investment project profitability. Representing the average cost of financing a company's operations, I learnt how WACC incorporates equity and debt capital costs. By comparing predicted project returns to the WACC, I was able to determine investment feasibility. To explore this further, it became clear to me that a company's sale can result in alterations to its risk profile. Such changes can influence the cost of equity, particularly if the sale affects the risk-free rate. This potential shift in risk dynamics is a critical factor in financial assessments.
Hence, I carried out future sales forecasts with the help of the Sales Operations Team, leveraging my knowledge of econometrics. After collecting data on daily sales from the previous year, I assessed data stability and stationarity through the KPSS and ADF tests, observing a relatively stable second-order difference in sales. Subsequently, I identified the optimal number of Auto-Regressive (p) and Moving Average terms (q) by aiming for low AIC and BIC values, optimising my model to maximise predictive power. With these parameters, I applied the ARMA model to predict future sales. During this process, I understood that the model needs to be updated regularly as new data becomes available, ensuring it does not suffer from overfitting. Studying Further Mathematics has helped me understand Gauss-Jordan elimination, which was beneficial for this exploration. I acknowledge that my current grasp of this subject matter is preliminary; however, I am excited to study it more deeply at the HKU Business School. Despite my model's high goodness of fit, I noticed that its vulnerability to exogenous shocks, like the COVID-19 pandemic, resulted in counter-intuitive outcomes. This experience emphasised the importance of sustainable growth and motivated me to deepen my understanding through Oxford's online courses. When introduced to the Solow growth model, I was intrigued by the conditional convergence hypothesis which states that nations, with similar parameters, converge in per capita income. The Golden Rule savings ratio, which maximises per capita consumption, also captivated my interest. Thereafter, I explored policy analysis, considering how nations facing dynamic efficiency might encourage savings through tax reform. Studying A-level Economics has further enlightened me about the multiplier effect and accelerator theory, offering an alternative insight into why investment stimulates economic growth. Adopting a multifaceted view allows me to thoroughly analyse these interconnected factors and provide nuanced policy guidance that can increase welfare.

As I continued investigating the Solow growth model and its impact on investment, I realised that domestic currency depreciation has the potential to stimulate investment. Immersing myself in "Introductory Econometrics for Finance," I grasped an understanding of panel data models. Afterwards, I embarked on my IPQ project centred on international macroeconomics, aiming to estimate exchange rate fluctuations. I systematically analysed underlying causes and utilised the Fixed Effects model to analyse the relationship between interest and exchange rates. By modelling the unobserved effects as two components, fixed effects and idiosyncratic errors, I used a first difference estimator to conduct causal inference. However, I realised that past macro shocks might affect future exchange rates, violating the assumption of strict exogeneity. To push myself, I applied the Arellano-Bond method to make robust estimation, where I used the interest rate in the first period as an instrument for the first difference in a later period. It was exciting to optimise statistical models to align them closer to underlying dynamics.
During my academic pursuits, I became fascinated with the volatile A-share market in China, spurring my interest in finance. Enrolling in modelling classes at a local university, I gained practical experience in stock price prediction and machine learning techniques. The course involved employing the Isolation Forest algorithm for outlier detection and the Support Vector Regression (SVR) model to predict the 'Shanghai Shenda' stock price, using data from April to June 2023 as the training set. Despite these efforts, the intrinsic volatility and unpredictability of financial markets posed challenges in achieving consistent alpha with the SVR model. Consequently, I shifted focus to reinforcement learning for portfolio optimization, which adapts portfolio allocations by learning from accumulated returns. This involved initializing Q-values in a Q-table to represent expected rewards for trading actions and making decisions based on historical asset prices. Each transaction resulted in a new portfolio state and performance-based rewards. Through the iterative process of updating Q-values, following the Bellman equation, I gained significant insights. This challenging exploration has only fuelled my passion to delve deeper into finance and machine learning at HKU business school.
Outside the classroom, I am proud to have earned a top 5% rank in the AMC, enhancing my mathematical skills and was awarded a Distinction in the John Locke essay competition, where I analysed the infla- tionary effects of QE. At HKU Business School, I am dedicated to leveraging data analytics to address practical challenges.
